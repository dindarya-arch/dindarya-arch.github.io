Din d'Arya

Independent researcher affiliated with the École Celtique, which refuses institutional recognition on principle. My work focuses on philological and epigraphic analysis of ancient Near Eastern texts, arguing that several documents conventionally dated to the ninth century BCE were in fact composed within the first-century BCE Herodian bilingual milieu. Core method: external contextualisation and convergence of interests, combined with systematic redating.

The Tel Dan Stele as a First-Century BCE Document: A Corpus of Philological Studies

This corpus brings together four philological and epigraphic studies arguing that the Tel Dan Stele is a first-century BCE document produced within the Herodian bilingual milieu, rather than an authentic ninth-century BCE Aramaic inscription. The studies address relative chronology, Koine Greek back-translation, intertextual analysis with the Psalms of Solomon, and a critique of the palaeographic consensus established by Demsky (1995).

The Tel-Dan Stele: A Roman Boundary Marker of Humiliation on the Via Dolorosa of Mattathias Antigonus II (Summer −37 BCE)
Din d'Arya · École Celtique · 2025 · Preprint · EN
Main preprint. The Tel Dan Stele is argued to have been erected in summer 37 BCE as a Roman boundary marker of humiliation on the Via Dolorosa of the last Hasmonean king Mattathias Antigonus II. Evidence rests on SVO word-order anomalies incompatible with authentic ninth-century Aramaic, with the Mesha Stele as a control corpus.
